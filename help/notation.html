There are several different kinds of chess notation available in WebChess. This page describes them and gives examples. Examples for all the notations can be compared in the table at the bottom of the page.


The following notations are available:

  • Coordinate
  • Algebraic
  • Long Algebraic
  • Descriptive
  • International
  • Verbose

Coordinate

Coordinate notation gives the starting square and the ending square as well as special notation for captures, en passant captures, castle moves, check, and mate moves.

Algebraic

Algebraic notation gives the piece being moved (if the piece is not a pawn) and part of the starting square only when the starting square is ambiguous (i.e. more than one piece could have moved to the ending square) as well as the ending square. Special notation for captures, en passant captures, castle moves, check, and mate moves is also given. This is the notation used in PGN files.

Long Algebraic

Long Algebraic notation is the same as Algebraic except the starting square is always given as a full square. This notation is the same as the (incorrectly named) PGN notation in previous versions of WebChess.

Descriptive

Descriptive notation gives the piece moved and the ending square as a descriptive location counted from the players own side. For instance: if a bishop moves from its home square to a square four spaces up in front of the queen side rook, then the notation is like this: B-R4, which means the bishop moved to the fourth square ABOVE the queens rook. If the other bishop could have moved into the fourth square above the king side rook, then the notation removes the ambiguity by adding queen side information like this: B-QR4. If the starting square is ambiguous, more information is given in the notation just as with ambiguous ending squares. For captures, only the moved piece and the captured piece are given as follows: NxR unless the move is ambiguous, then more information is given. Your opponents back rank is 8, your own back rank is 1. Special notation for captures, en passant captures, castle moves, check, and mate moves is also given. Because this notation is based on the starting positions in an orthodox game, this notation may get a little confusing in Chess960 games.

International

International notation gives the starting square and the ending square as a four digit number in the format: starting file, starting rank, ending file, ending rank. All numbers are counted 1-8 from the left-hand, white-side dark square (A1). For instance, a move from the 3rd file, 4th rank, to the 5th file, 5th rank would be displayed as 3455. No special notation is given for captures, en passant captures, castle moves, check, or mate. For castle moves, the notation follows the king. For a-side castles (long castle) it would be 5131. Because of this, castle move notation may get confusing in Chess960 games because the king may only move one square, and it becomes ambiguous if this move was a castle move, or a normal move. For pawn promotions the notation is a little different. When a pawn is promoted, the final number (the ending rank) is replaced with a numerical representation of the piece the pawn was promoted to, such as: 4742, which would be a pawn moving along file 4 from rank 7 to rank 8 (not shown because it is understood), and promoted to a rook (2). Q=1, R=2, B=3, N=4. If the move had been a pawn capture and promotion, the notation would be: 4751. Capturing the piece on file 5, and promoted to queen.

Verbose

Verbose notation is just like regular speech: "pawn from a2 to a4", "queen from d5 captures g2, check".
